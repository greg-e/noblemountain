## 1. Overview

Published in 1908, *How to Live on 24 Hours a Day* is a short essay by Arnold Bennett arguing that most people squander the "day within the day" — the roughly 16 waking hours beyond their obligatory working hours. Bennett's central claim is simple: **the average knowledge worker of his era had three hours each evening and weekend mornings that, if used with deliberate intention, could transform their inner and outer life.** He was not prescribing a grind-culture hustle routine; he was prescribing *concentrated, planned attention* as the mechanism for self-development.

Bennett wrote before cognitive psychology, neuroscience, or evidence-based time-management existed as fields. Yet several of his intuitions anticipate modern findings with surprising precision, while others reflect the blind spots of his era — class privilege, the invisible labor of domestic life, and an almost complete ignorance of fatigue physiology.

This document performs three tasks:

1. **Deconstruct** Bennett's principles with fidelity to his text.
2. **Stress-test** each principle against current research (behavioral psychology, time management science, cognitive load theory, chronobiology, and fatigue research).
3. **Translate** the valid core into a concrete, measurable daily system, with explicit handling of the edge cases Bennett ignored.

## 2. Core Principles

### 2.1 The "Day Within the Day"

Bennett observes that a typical office worker of 1908 spent 7.5 hours commuting and working, 8 hours sleeping, and considered the remaining 8.5 hours as leisure — and therefore not "their" time in any serious sense. He reframes the remaining hours as a second life available for cultivation.

**Key claim:** You already have enough time. The scarcity is not hours but *intentional allocation*.

### 2.2 The Morning Commute as Contemplative Space

Bennett recommends using the morning commute (30–90 minutes) for structured mental exercise: focused reflection on a philosophical or ethical question carried from the prior evening. He explicitly warns against reading the newspaper — passive consumption of others' thought — and advocates for active internal dialogue.

**Key claim:** Transitions and liminal time (commute, waiting, walking) can be used for deep, non-distracted reflection.

### 2.3 The 90-Minute Evening Block

Bennett recommends carving out three evenings per week (specifically, not all five) for a 90-minute block of deliberate, concentrated study or practice. He insists on regularity over intensity: the same days, the same time, the same activity type. He explicitly argues against trying to do too much, because over-commitment leads to collapse and abandonment.

**Key claim:** Sustainable practice requires scheduling *less* than you think you can handle, with regularity as the primary variable.

### 2.4 Concentration as a Trainable Skill

Bennett argues that the inability to focus is not a fixed trait but a habit of mind that can be changed through exercise. He recommends starting with 15 minutes of undivided attention on a single topic — no fidgeting, no mind-wandering permitted — and treating this as a physical training exercise.

**Key claim:** Attention is a capacity that degrades with misuse and strengthens with practice.

### 2.5 Interest Precedes Discipline

Bennett is surprisingly non-prescriptive about *what* to study. He insists that genuine interest — not duty — must drive topic selection, because discipline without intrinsic motivation collapses quickly. He recommends poetry, philosophy, music, and the arts specifically because they engage the whole person.

**Key claim:** Sustained engagement requires intrinsic motivation. External duty is too brittle to maintain over years.

### 2.6 Cause and Effect Thinking

One of Bennett's most specific exercises is to pick one area of life (relationships, habits, work quality) and spend time each week deliberately tracing outcomes back to their causes. He frames this as building a causal model of one's own behavior.

**Key claim:** Systematic causal reflection on personal behavior produces better self-regulation than vague journaling or passive experience.

### 2.7 The Anti-Perfectionism Rule

Bennett explicitly warns against "taking care of the time by the day" (i.e., trying to optimize every single hour). He argues for "taking care of the day by the week" — accepting that some days will be wasted, so long as the weekly rhythm is maintained.

**Key claim:** The unit of habit maintenance is the week, not the day. Perfectionism at the daily level causes the same over-commitment failure as trying to do too much.

## 3. Alignment with Modern Research

### 3.1 Time Scarcity Is Largely Perceptual — Behavioral Psychology

Bennett's core premise — that people have more discretionary time than they perceive — is supported by time-use research. The American Time Use Survey (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2023) consistently shows that full-time employed adults average 4.9–5.5 hours of leisure per weekday, the majority of which is consumed by passive television and screen use.

**Research alignment:** Cassie Mogilner Holmes et al. (2012, *Psychological Science*) demonstrated that giving time away — volunteering, focused activity — paradoxically *increases* perceived time affluence. Bennett's prescription to fill discretionary time with intention produces the subjective experience of having *more* time, not less. This is the opposite of the intuitive model.

### 3.2 Habit Formation and the Regularity Principle

Bennett's insistence on fixed-day, fixed-time practice anticipates implementation intention research. Peter Gollwitzer's work (1999) shows that pre-deciding *when, where, and how* an action will be performed ("On Monday at 7pm, I will sit at my desk and read chapter 3") increases follow-through by 200–300% compared to goal-only intentions.

**Research alignment:** Bennett's three fixed evenings per week maps precisely onto the implementation-intention format. His warning against doing all five nights also anticipates ego-depletion and decision fatigue concerns (Baumeister et al., 1998, though later replication studies have complicated the mechanistic claim, the behavioral effect of over-scheduling is well-documented in self-regulation research).

### 3.3 Deliberate Practice and Skill Acquisition

Bennett's description of concentration training — progressively extending focused attention from 15 minutes, treating failure to focus as a training error to correct — closely parallels Anders Ericsson's deliberate practice framework (1993). Ericsson's studies of expert performers show that quality of practice (focused, effortful, feedback-rich) predicts skill acquisition more reliably than total hours.

**Research alignment:** Bennett's attention-training exercise is essentially a proto-version of meditation-based attention training. Mindfulness-based attention training (MBAT) studies (Jha et al., 2010) show that 12 minutes of daily focused-attention practice measurably improves working memory capacity and reduces attentional failures in high-stress populations.

### 3.4 Cognitive Load and the Liminal-Time Prescription

Bennett's commute prescription — sustained reflection on a single pre-loaded question — is well-supported by cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988). The commute is low cognitive-load for a habituated route; this spare capacity can be redirected to deliberate internal processing without the kind of divided-attention cost that kills deep thinking in high-load environments.

**Research alignment:** Mind-wandering research (Smallwood & Schooler, 2015) distinguishes between *unintentional* mind-wandering (associated with anxiety and reduced performance) and *deliberate* prospective thought (associated with creative problem-solving). Bennett's commute exercise is explicitly the second type: intentionally directed, question-seeded internal dialogue — closer to incubation than to distraction.

### 3.5 Interest and Intrinsic Motivation

Bennett's insistence that topic selection must be driven by genuine interest — not duty — anticipates Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985). SDT distinguishes between intrinsic motivation (activity is inherently rewarding), identified regulation (activity is valued for personal growth), and external regulation (activity is done for reward or to avoid punishment). SDT research consistently shows that intrinsically motivated practice produces superior learning, persistence, and well-being compared to externally driven equivalents.

**Research alignment:** Bennett's framework is implicitly an autonomy-supportive design. His rejection of rigid prescriptions about *what* to study is theoretically sound — people are more likely to sustain self-chosen activities over years.

### 3.6 Weekly vs. Daily Habit Management

Bennett's "take care of the week, not the day" rule anticipates what habit researchers call *abstraction level* in goal representation. Research by Fujita et al. (2006) shows that high-level (weekly, monthly) goal representation produces more flexible, robust behavior than low-level (daily, hourly) representation, which is brittle under disruption.

**Research alignment:** The weekly rhythm also intersects with work by Seinfeld's "don't break the chain" popularization of habit streaks — though notably, modern habit research (Gardner et al., 2012) confirms that missing *occasional* days does not significantly harm habit formation as long as the overall frequency is maintained. Bennett anticipated this empirically.

## 4. Where Bennett Falls Short

### 4.1 Complete Ignorance of Fatigue Physiology

Bennett assumes that the evening hours after a day's work are freely available for intellectual effort. This ignores sleep pressure (Process S in the two-process model of sleep regulation, Borbély, 1982). By 9–10pm for most adults with a 6–7am wake time, adenosine accumulation has already significantly degraded executive function, working memory, and sustained attention.

**The failure:** Bennett's prescription — starting concentrated study at 9pm after an 8–10 hour workday — systematically assigns the highest-demand cognitive tasks to the worst cognitive time window. For chronobiologically normal adults (intermediate chronotype), peak cognitive performance occurs in the late morning (9–11am), with a secondary window in the early afternoon. Evening performance is substantially below peak.

**Evidence:** Hahn et al. (2012) showed that cognitive flexibility and working memory performance decline 15–25% in the evening compared to morning. Creative insight problem-solving shows the opposite pattern (the "incubation" effect favors off-peak hours for some problem types), but focused, analytical study of the kind Bennett prescribes does not.

### 4.2 Invisible Labor and the Myth of "Free" Evening Time

Bennett writes from the position of a single professional man in 1908 with domestic servants and no childcare obligations. He assumes that the 8.5 hours outside work and sleep are genuinely discretionary.

**The failure:** For the majority of adults — particularly parents of young children, primary caregivers, and those without domestic support — the "free" hours after work are already consumed by cooking, cleaning, school preparation, child bedtime, household administration, and emotional support work. Time-use research (Hochschild, *The Second Shift*, 1989; BLS ATUS 2023) shows that working parents average 2.0–2.5 hours of discretionary time per weekday, not 8.5, and that this discretionary time is non-contiguous and fragmented.

**Evidence:** Non-contiguous leisure time is substantially less restorative and less usable for deep work than the same duration of contiguous time (Trougakos et al., 2014). Bennett's 90-minute block requires a contiguous window that most parents of children under 10 simply cannot reliably generate on weekday evenings.

### 4.3 No Theory of Rest or Recovery

Bennett treats rest as passive — sleeping is simply the absence of activity. He provides no positive account of *what* makes rest restorative, or *why* certain types of leisure are more cognitively recuperative than others.

**The failure:** Modern recovery science (Sonnentag & Fritz, 2007) identifies four mechanisms of work recovery: *psychological detachment* (mentally disconnecting from work demands), *relaxation* (low-arousal positive affect), *mastery* (skill-building in non-work domains), and *control* (autonomy over one's leisure). Bennett's evening block provides mastery and control but not detachment or relaxation — it replaces one form of cognitive demand with another. For workers experiencing high occupational stress, adding a concentrated evening study block without an explicit recovery protocol risks chronic under-recovery and eventual burnout.

### 4.4 Willpower as an Infinite Resource

Bennett's framework implicitly assumes that self-control and concentration are available on demand, subject only to the *decision* to exercise them. He has no model of cognitive resource depletion across the day.

**The failure:** While the original Baumeister ego-depletion model has faced replication challenges, the broader finding that decision load, emotional labor, and executive function demands earlier in the day impair subsequent self-regulation is robust (Hagger et al., 2010, meta-analysis; Dang et al., 2017). Workers in high-decision, high-social-interaction roles (managers, teachers, healthcare workers, customer service staff) arrive home with demonstrably impaired self-regulatory capacity, regardless of subjective tiredness.

### 4.5 No Provision for Unpredictable Work

Bennett writes in an era of fixed working hours (he explicitly uses a 9.5-hour work day). He has no mechanism for the unpredictable schedule intrusions of modern knowledge work: late meetings, crisis management, deadlines that extend into the evening, business travel, and the always-on communication expectations created by smartphone connectivity.

**The failure:** The fixed evening block that anchors his system requires protected time that many workers cannot reliably guarantee. A system with no contingency protocol fails as soon as the first disruption occurs — and for modern knowledge workers, disruption is not the exception but the baseline.

### 4.6 Social and Relational Dimensions Ignored

Bennett's "evening of self-development" is explicitly solitary. He does not account for the restorative and motivational value of social connection, nor for the legitimate demands of partnership, friendship, and family that compete with solitary study time.

**The failure:** Social connection is one of the most robust predictors of psychological well-being (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015) and cognitive health across the lifespan. A productivity system that consistently deprioritizes social connection in favor of solitary self-cultivation creates a hidden cost that compounds over years.

## 5. The Concrete Daily System

The following system preserves Bennett's valid core — intentional allocation, fixed rhythms, progressive concentration training, intrinsic topic selection — while addressing the failures identified in Section 4.

### 5.1 Design Principles

| Principle | Source |
|---|---|
| Use *chronotype-aligned* focus blocks, not arbitrary evening time | Chronobiology, Hahn et al. (2012) |
| Protect *one* fixed 60–90 minute block, not three evening blocks | Implementation intention, Gollwitzer (1999) |
| Separate recovery from practice | Sonnentag & Fritz (2007) |
| Use weekly resilience, not daily perfection | Fujita et al. (2006) |
| Make intrinsic interest the selection criterion | SDT, Deci & Ryan (1985) |
| Build in a contingency protocol for disrupted days | Behavioral flexibility, modern habit research |

### 5.2 The System: Four Structural Elements

---

#### Element 1 — The Morning Anchor (15–20 minutes)

**What:** Before entering reactive work (email, Slack, calendar), complete a single cognitive act of your choosing — writing, reading in your chosen domain, a brief planning review, or the concentration exercise Bennett describes (holding one question in mind for 15 minutes without distraction).

**When:** Immediately after waking, before any device that carries incoming messages. For most adults, this is the highest-alertness window with the lowest prior decision load.

**Why it works:** Morning executive function is near its daily peak for most chronotypes. The "no devices first" rule eliminates attentional capture by incoming demands, which research shows degrades the quality of subsequent focused work for up to 1 hour (Newport, *Deep Work*, 2016; Kushlev & Dunn, 2015, on email frequency and stress).

**Measurable output:** Days per week that the anchor was completed (target: 5/7).

---

#### Element 2 — The Weekly Practice Block (60–90 minutes, 2–3 times per week)

**What:** A non-negotiable, calendar-blocked session devoted to the one self-development project you have selected based on genuine interest. This is Bennett's core evening prescription, shifted to the highest-quality time available to you.

**When:** Determined individually by chronotype assessment (MEQ or Munich Chronotype Questionnaire). For morning types: before work. For evening types: early evening (6–7pm, before significant sleep pressure accumulates). For intermediate types: late morning on weekends, or immediately after work before domestic demands begin.

**Why it works:** Matching the highest cognitive demand (concentrated study) to the highest-quality cognitive window is the adjustment Bennett's system most critically needs. A 60-minute block at peak cognitive time outperforms a 90-minute block at trough time for analytical and learning tasks.

**Contingency protocol:** If the scheduled session is disrupted (meeting overrun, child illness, travel), the week is *not* treated as failed. One disruption is absorbed. Two disruptions in one week trigger a 20-minute "minimum viable" session on the nearest available day — enough to maintain the habit signal without abandoning the week.

**Measurable output:** Practice sessions completed per week (target: 2 of 3 scheduled); accumulated hours per month.

---

#### Element 3 — The Commute or Transition Reflection (15–30 minutes)

**What:** Exactly as Bennett prescribes: one pre-loaded question or topic, held in mind without passive media consumption. This can be the unsolved problem from the morning anchor, a conceptual question from the practice block, or a causal-reflection prompt ("Why did I respond the way I did in that meeting?").

**When:** During any regular transition — commute, walk, exercise — where attention is free and the activity is habituated (no new navigation, no social obligation).

**Why it works:** This is the incubation mechanism from creativity research (Sio & Ormerod, 2009). Directed but low-demand processing of a pre-loaded problem significantly increases insight solution rates compared to either focused problem-solving or passive distraction. The commute is structurally ideal because habituated navigation frees attention without creating a quiet environment that requires active maintenance.

**Measurable output:** Days per week the transition was used intentionally (target: 3/5 commuting days); qualitative log entry of one insight or thought per session (5 minutes, end of commute).

---

#### Element 4 — The Weekly Review (20–30 minutes, Sunday or Monday morning)

**What:** A structured 20-minute review of the previous week's practice sessions, anchor completions, and one identified causal relationship (Bennett's "cause and effect" exercise applied to a real event from the week). Set intentions for the next week's three practice sessions.

**When:** Sunday evening or Monday morning, before the week begins.

**Why it works:** Weekly review closes the feedback loop that turns behavior into skill. Without explicit review, the "learning by doing" assumption embedded in Bennett's system — that regular practice automatically produces self-improvement — fails, because humans are poor at extracting lessons from experience without deliberate reflection (Deakin et al., 2018, on deliberate reflection and performance improvement).

**Measurable output:** Review completed (yes/no); one specific insight recorded; practice sessions planned for the coming week (calendar entries present).

---

### 5.3 Daily Template

```
MORNING (30–35 min before reactive work)
  [15 min] Morning anchor: concentration exercise / writing / domain reading
  [15 min] Day-shaping: top-3 priorities, protect practice block time
  [5 min]  Transition reflection seed: load one question to carry

WORK DAY
  [as scheduled] Practice block (if morning-type or weekend — move here)
  [commute/walk] Transition reflection: hold pre-loaded question

EVENING (if intermediate/evening chronotype)
  [60–90 min, 2–3 evenings per week] Practice block
  [20–30 min] Social / restorative buffer before practice (decompression)
  [protect] Hard stop time: no practice block starting within 90 min of target sleep time

WEEKLY (Sunday or Monday, 20–30 min)
  [10 min] Review: sessions completed, one causal insight
  [10 min] Plan: schedule next week's 3 practice blocks on calendar
  [5 min]  Adjust: is the topic still intrinsically motivating? Is the schedule realistic?
```

### 5.4 Selecting the Practice Domain

Bennett's only guidance is "genuine interest." The following decision filter operationalizes this:

1. **The 3-year test:** Would you still want to be working on this in 3 years if no external reward existed? If uncertain, start with a 4-week trial and reassess.
2. **The teaching test:** Can you explain what you are learning to someone else? Inability to explain is a signal that passive consumption has replaced active learning.
3. **The resistance test:** Notice whether resistance before sessions is anxious ("I should be doing something else") or generative ("I'm not sure I can handle this material"). Only generative resistance signals that the domain is at the right difficulty level for skill acquisition (Ericsson's zone of proximal development equivalent).

## 6. Critiques, Limitations, and Edge Cases

### 6.1 Fatigue and Sleep Debt

**Problem:** Any system built on discretionary time outside paid work assumes adequate baseline sleep. The CDC reports that 35% of US adults regularly sleep fewer than 7 hours per night (CDC, 2016), with knowledge workers and caregivers disproportionately represented. For sleep-deprived individuals, adding a focused-practice block does not produce skill development; it accelerates cognitive deterioration.

**System response:** Sleep takes priority over practice. The rule is: if you are averaging fewer than 7 hours per night over the current week, the practice block is replaced by sleep. No exceptions. This is not a failure of discipline — it is physiologically correct prioritization. A well-rested person practicing 3 hours per week will outperform a sleep-deprived person practicing 6 hours per week in virtually every cognitive skill domain (Walker, *Why We Sleep*, 2017).

**Bennett's failure:** He does not name this. His implicit assumption is that sleep is fixed and leisure time is the variable. Modern evidence inverts this: sleep is the most important variable, and leisure/practice time is the residual.

---

### 6.2 Shift Work, Non-Standard Schedules, and Irregular Hours

**Problem:** Bennett's entire system assumes a stable, predictable schedule. Roughly 16% of US workers are shift workers (BLS, 2023), and a significantly higher percentage of knowledge workers experience schedule variability driven by time-zone collaboration, on-call requirements, and project deadlines.

**System response:** Replace fixed-time practice blocks with *fixed-condition* triggers. Instead of "Monday at 7pm," the trigger becomes "the first 60-minute window available after sleep and before a 3-hour contiguous demand block." This is behaviorally less reliable than time-based implementation intentions but is more resilient under schedule disruption. For shift workers, the weekly review becomes the primary structural tool — working backwards from each week's known schedule to identify in advance the best 2–3 windows.

---

### 6.3 Parents and Primary Caregivers

**Problem:** Parents of children under 10, and primary caregivers of dependents, face two interacting constraints: (1) evening hours are heavily consumed by domestic labor and child bedtime, and (2) available time is non-contiguous and subject to interruption — conditions that preclude the concentrated, uninterrupted practice block Bennett describes.

**System response:** Three adjustments:

1. **Shift to early morning.** For parents with children who sleep past 6am, a 5:15–6:30am window is often genuinely protected time. The cost is earlier sleep, which must be non-negotiable.
2. **Reduce session length.** A 30-minute uninterrupted session is more valuable than a 60-minute session that is interrupted twice. Use the contingency protocol to define 30 minutes as the minimum viable session for caregiving phases of life.
3. **Involve partners.** Negotiate one evening per week as protected practice time for each partner independently. This reframes the practice block as a household structural commitment rather than a unilateral personal preference, reducing the conflict load.

**Bennett's failure:** He was blind to this constraint entirely. The system as written is inaccessible to approximately 50% of the adult working population without significant structural modification.

---

### 6.4 Mental Health Conditions

**Problem:** For individuals managing depression, anxiety disorders, ADHD, or burnout, Bennett's prescriptions interact with symptom profiles in complex ways. Depression-associated anhedonia can make intrinsic interest impossible to access. ADHD makes sustained attention training possible but requires different implementation (shorter sessions, external accountability, body-doubling). Burnout makes any additional structured demand counterproductive during the acute phase.

**System response:**

- **Depression:** During acute phases, replace the practice block with behavioral activation — any mildly pleasant physical activity counts as the session. The structural habit (showing up at the scheduled time) is preserved; the cognitive demand is removed.
- **ADHD:** Use external accountability (working in a library, study group, or body-double video call). Reduce session length to 25 minutes (one Pomodoro). Use visible timers. The concentration exercise Bennett prescribes is well-matched to ADHD if scaffolded correctly — it is essentially attention training with a single well-defined target.
- **Burnout:** Apply a minimum 4-week recovery protocol (Maslach & Leiter, 2008) before introducing structured practice. During recovery, the system runs only at Element 4 (weekly review) with no practice block demands.

---

### 6.5 The Productivity Trap

**Problem:** Bennett's system, and especially modern reframings of it, can be captured by productivity culture — the pathological orientation toward self-optimization that research links to anxiety, chronic dissatisfaction, and deteriorating relationships (Gregg, *Work's Intimacy*, 2011; Schulte, *Overwhelmed*, 2014).

**Safeguard:** The system includes an explicit *anti-goals* constraint: the weekly review must include assessment of whether the practice block is crowding out unstructured social time. If social connection (non-task-oriented time with family, friends, or community) has fallen below 7 hours per week, the practice block frequency is reduced by one session until the balance is restored.

**Bennett's failure:** His framework has no social dimension and no explicit upper bound on self-development effort. This omission is dangerous when the framework is adopted by people already predisposed to over-achievement.

---

### 6.6 The Sustainability Horizon

**Problem:** Bennett does not specify a timeframe for his system to produce results, which creates a failure mode: abandonment when progress is not visible on the wrong timescale.

**Expectation calibration based on evidence:**

| Skill domain | Minimum hours to measurable competency improvement | Evidence base |
|---|---|---|
| Language learning (basic conversational) | 150–200 hours | Foreign Service Institute data |
| Musical instrument (basic pieces) | 100–150 hours | Ericsson (1993); Sloboda et al. |
| Programming (basic fluency) | 200–400 hours | Multiple meta-analyses |
| Writing (measurable style improvement) | 50–100 hours deliberate practice + feedback | Kellogg & Wheatley (2012) |
| Meditation / attention training | 8–12 hours (8-week MBSR protocol) | Jha et al. (2010) |

At 2 sessions × 60 minutes × 52 weeks = 104 hours per year, the system yields approximately 2 years to basic competency in most skill domains. This is Bennett's implicit promise — and it is roughly accurate by modern standards. Managing this expectation explicitly prevents the disillusionment that causes early abandonment.

## 7. Measurement and Testing Protocol

The following metrics operationalize the system for empirical personal testing. All metrics should be tracked weekly in a simple log (paper, spreadsheet, or note).

### 7.1 Compliance Metrics (Leading Indicators)

| Metric | Definition | Target | Minimum |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anchor rate | Morning anchors completed / 5 weekdays | 5/5 | 3/5 |
| Practice session rate | Sessions completed / sessions scheduled | 3/3 | 2/3 |
| Session duration | Median session length (minutes) | 60 | 30 |
| Transition reflection rate | Intentional commute reflections / commuting days | 5/5 | 3/5 |
| Weekly review rate | Reviews completed / weeks | 1/1 | 3/4 |
| Sleep baseline | Nights ≥ 7 hours / 7 days | 7/7 | 5/7 |

### 7.2 Output Metrics (Lagging Indicators, reviewed monthly)

| Metric | Measurement method | Review frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Skill domain progress | Domain-specific test or output sample | Monthly |
| Subjective energy rating | 1–10 scale, end of each practice session | Weekly |
| Perceived time affluence | Single-item scale (Mogilner Holmes) | Monthly |
| Social connection hours | Hours of non-task social time per week | Weekly |
| Session resistance type | Anxious vs. generative (qualitative) | Per session |

### 7.3 Adjustment Triggers

The system is adjusted (not abandoned) when the following thresholds are crossed:

- **Anchor rate < 3/5 for 3 consecutive weeks:** Remove the morning anchor; shift its function to the practice block opening.
- **Practice session rate < 2/3 for 4 consecutive weeks:** Reduce scheduled sessions from 3 to 2 per week and reassess time slot.
- **Subjective energy rating ≤ 4 for 3 consecutive sessions:** Shift to activation-only sessions (physical activity, low-demand reading) for 2 weeks; add one additional sleep-priority night per week.
- **Social connection < 7 hours for 3 consecutive weeks:** Reduce practice sessions by one per week until corrected.
- **Sleep baseline < 5/7 for 2 consecutive weeks:** Suspend practice block entirely; restore sleep baseline; restart at reduced session frequency.
